Who's Liable When AI Gets It Wrong?
LinkedIn PENDING APPROVAL
English Text
When a self-driving car kills someone — who faces criminal liability? The car? The algorithm? Tesla? The passenger who was reading the newspaper? Now replace "car" with "AI system that advised you on a complex transaction" — and you'll find yourself standing in exactly the same legal vacuum. In the generative AI era, three questions define your legal exposure: 1. Who owns the output? The firm that bought the license? The client who fed in the data? The developer who built the model? Nobody? 2. Who bears liability when that output causes damage? Is "the tool recommended it" a valid legal defense? (Hint: it's not.) 3. Can a decision-making process be legally valid when it's a black box? A black box doesn't testify in court. You do. The DOM principle is unambiguous: A decision without comprehension is not a decision — it's a guess wearing a suit. Actionable insight for senior practitioners: Every contract where AI is involved needs an explicit transparency clause: who made the decision, based on what data, and who is accountable if it's wrong. The liability gap in AI-assisted work isn't a future problem. It's already sitting in your files. The question is whether you've drafted around it — or into it. #LegalAlgorist #AILaw #IntellectualProperty #AILiability #FutureOfLaw #RiskManagement #DOM #GenerativeAI #LegalTech #ContractDrafting
